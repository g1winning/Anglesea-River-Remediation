Our Beloved River is Hurting

The Anglesea River and estuary have suffered repeated acidification events from exposed acid sulfate soils, worsened by past mining and drying peat swamps. Low pH, toxic aluminium and iron releases, and floc precipitates have caused fish kills, harmed aquatic life, and threatened the health of this iconic Surf Coast waterway we all love surfing, fishing, walking, and sharing with our kids.

The community is worried – about groundwater depletion, ongoing acidity pulses, and the long-term health of our estuary. We need a gentle, nature-based solution that works with stakeholders and the landscape, not against it.

Biochar: Nature's Ancient Soil Healer

Biochar is a stable, carbon-rich material made by slowly heating local woody waste in low-oxygen conditions (pyrolysis). Used by Indigenous peoples for millennia to enrich soils, modern biochar is a powerful tool for regeneration.

It neutralises acidity, binds toxic metals, holds water like a sponge (helping keep swamps wetter longer), sequesters carbon for centuries, and improves soil life. Produced locally using the efficient Kon-Tiki flame curtain kiln – a low-investment, community-friendly method.

A Permaculture-Inspired, Whole-of-Landscape Plan

This community-led approach applies permaculture principles: observe, slow the flow, use local resources, and regenerate from source to sea. Every step creates jobs, builds community skills, and draws down carbon.

Step 1: Community Biochar Production

We produce high-quality eucalyptus biochar (>85% carbon) locally using the Kon-Tiki flame curtain kiln – a simple, efficient, low-investment method that's safe and accessible for community use. Training and employing locals to process park thinning waste and greenwaste creates jobs, fosters hands-on forest engagement (making parks safer and more accessible), reduces fire hazard, and sequesters carbon immediately.

Step 2: Source Control in the Acid Swamps

Shallow incorporation of biochar (20–40 tonnes/ha) into upper catchment acid swamps. Raises soil pH, retains moisture to suppress sulfide oxidation, immobilises metals at source – preventing acidic pulses before they start.

Step 3: Slow-the-Flow Swales

Dig gentle contour swales uphill, infilled with 10–20% biochar. Slows runoff, promotes deep infiltration, recharges groundwater, reduces erosion and exposure of new sulfidic soils. Overflow points fitted with biochar filters to prevent acidic runoff.

Step 4: Treatment Filters at Coalmine Road

Modular biochar filters at the key Coalmine Road drainage chokepoint. Treats acidic water before it reaches the estuary – removing 70–99% of toxic aluminium and iron, raising pH above safe levels. Passive, low-maintenance, lasting 5–10 years.

Step 5: Healthy Waterways & Thriving Estuary

The result: stable pH >5.5, dramatically reduced metal toxicity, no more floc events. Native fish and invertebrates return, aquatic plants flourish, and the whole ecosystem rebounds. A resilient, healthy river for generations.
